Risk and Hazard Identification and Control: Building a Safer Workplace

By EMTAGHub101 — Published: November 10, 2025

Introduction

Effective workplace safety begins with knowing what can harm people — recognizing hazards — and then assessing the risk those hazards present so controls can be applied to prevent harm. This article dives deep into the processes and practicalities of risk and hazard identification and control across industries. It provides concrete examples for each category of hazard, tools for assessment, and clear action steps for implementing controls that protect employees and meet legal responsibilities.

Definitions — Hazard vs Risk

A hazard is anything with potential to cause harm. A risk is the combination of the probability that the harm will occur and the severity of that harm. Distinguishing hazard from risk ensures that workplaces prioritize the most pressing safety problems.

Hazard Categories & Examples

Hazards can be grouped by type; each category requires different detection methods and control strategies. Below are major categories with practical examples:

Physical Hazards

  • Machinery entanglement: Exposed rotating shafts on a production line. Control: Install guards, emergency stops, and lockout/tagout procedures.
  • Falling objects: Tools stored on elevated racks. Control: Use toe boards and exclusion zones to prevent access below elevated tools.
  • Noise: Heavy presses producing >85 dB over a shift. Control: Install noise dampening materials, rotate workers, and provide hearing protection.

Chemical Hazards

  • Toxic fumes: Use of solvent-based cleaners in poorly ventilated spaces. Control: Substitute with low-VOC products, use local exhaust ventilation, or respirators.
  • Corrosives: Acid storage near walkways. Control: Implement secondary containment and clearly mark the area for restricted access.

Ergonomic Hazards

  • Repetitive strain: Assembly tasks with repeated wrist motion. Control: Implement job rotation, ergonomic tools, and micro-breaks to reduce strain.
  • Manual handling: Lifting heavy boxes without aids. Control: Use mechanical aids, or implement a team-lifting policy with training.

Conclusion

Risk and hazard identification and control are central to creating safe workplaces. Through systematic inspection, accurate risk assessment, applying the hierarchy of controls, and continuous monitoring, organisations can reduce incidents and build a safety-first culture. Investing in safety pays in reduced injuries, higher productivity, and stronger reputations.
